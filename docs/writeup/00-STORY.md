# 00 — The Story

The personal narrative that anchors the submission. The video, the writeup, and the track framing all depend on getting this right. More important than any single feature.

*Adapted from the Kaggle-era story doc. The verified facts are unchanged. What changed is the thesis it serves: not a coordination network for displaced makers, but a system that keeps expert making-knowledge from disappearing.*

---

## What is true (verified — use without qualification)

- Your grandfather was born in Guatemala City on May 14, 1936.
- He came from a family of businesspeople; you were told they were a wealthy family with business operations.
- He worked for the family business before immigrating to the US in 1960, via Louisiana.
- He attended Chamberlain Hunt Academy and the University of Southern Mississippi.
- He was an Electrical Engineering Lab Instructor at Lamar University (then Port Arthur College) for 12 years.
- He became a US citizen in December 1973.
- He spent his career as a Communications Engineer for the City of Beaumont, TX, retiring in 2000.
- His obituary notes he enjoyed tinkering in his shop.
- You spent time in that shop as a kid — you loved being there, making things.
- Alzheimer's took him. The shop was sold. He was moved to your uncle's home. He died in hospice.

## What is NOT verified (do not use as fact)

- "Invented a system to print labels on thread spools" — family oral history, not confirmed.
- "For textile factories in Guatemala" — same uncertainty.
- Any specific manufacturing activity in Guatemala.

You saw a machine. You were told the family had a business. You don't know exactly what the machine did. The true story doesn't need those specifics — it's stronger without them.

## Why the true story is better

Specificity you can't verify is a liability. The true story has something better: **it's about loss.** Not abstract displacement — your grandfather's actual shop, your actual childhood, his actual decline, the dispersal of his tools and his accumulated skill. That's the emotional center. Judges will remember it. No other submission will have anything like it.

## The narrative (basis for writeup + video — your voice, adjust wording)

> My grandfather had a shop. He was an electrical engineer by training, but at home he was a tinkerer — a home RadioShack with a machine shop attached. I spent time in that shop as a kid, making things, watching him build. He had tools I didn't understand and projects in every state of done. It was one of my favorite places.
>
> He spent a lifetime accumulating skill — the kind that lives in your hands, that you can't quite write down. Then Alzheimer's took him. The shop was sold, his tools dispersed, and all of that knowledge just… went. There was no way to capture it. No way to keep it.
>
> The Chief Engineer is my attempt at the opposite of that. It's a small AI that runs on my own machine and learns the craft of 3D printing the way a shop veteran would — job by job, remembering what worked in which conditions, getting better every time. It compresses expert knowledge so it compounds instead of disappearing. When I start a print, it doesn't wait for me to fail — it looks at what it's learned and tells me where this one will go wrong before it does.
>
> It runs locally, on consumer hardware, for free. Because the knowledge a maker builds over a lifetime shouldn't depend on a cloud subscription — and it shouldn't vanish when the shop closes.

## How to use it

**In the video (open, ~0:00–0:30):** Tell it on camera, your pace. Beats: (1) the shop, you loved it; (2) he was skilled — an EE, a tinkerer; (3) Alzheimer's, the shop sold, the knowledge gone; (4) that's why this exists. Don't rush the grief — judges lean in. That's what you want at second 10 of a short video.

**In the writeup (open, ≤15% of words):** A compressed 3–4 sentence version as the opening, establishing stakes before the technical description. Emotional setup first, then the build.

**Track framing — Backyard AI:** The problem is real and specific (you, a maker, want expert-level print guidance you don't have). You actually use it. The fit between the problem and a small local model is honest (capturing and recalling craft knowledge is exactly what a compact model + a good memory does). The grandfather is the *why*, not the user — the reason the project is about knowledge that persists.

---

## What the story does NOT need to be

- **Not about Guatemala specifically.** His heritage is real and worth a mention for authenticity, but don't build the technical story on the unverifiable business claim.
- **Not an abstraction** ("1M displaced workers"). You have a specific person. Use him.
- **Don't soften the Alzheimer's and hospice.** The hard truth is why the project matters. Softening it loses the reason.
- **Don't make him a myth.** He was an engineer who liked to tinker, not a legendary inventor. The ordinary truth is more relatable.

## The closing line (video)

> "The shop is gone. But the kind of knowledge that lived in it — built up over a lifetime, lost in an afternoon — doesn't have to disappear anymore."

Adjust to your voice. Close on the human point, not a technical claim.
